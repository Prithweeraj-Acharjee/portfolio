I am writing this from the other side of a season I was not sure I would walk out of.

A few weeks ago I was suspended from the university I came to America to attend. I am here on an F-1 visa, and that piece of paper is the only reason I am allowed to be inside this country at all. Without an enrolled student status, the visa goes. Without the visa, I go. I had about three weeks to fix it, and inside those three weeks were two weekends I could not work, a national holiday, a transfer school admissions packet, an appeal letter, a replacement I-20, and the worst silence I have ever felt — the silence of waiting on other people's emails.

I made it through. The appeal was approved yesterday. I am being reinstated into University College for Fall 2026, on conditions I will gladly carry. The transfer school is no longer needed. The visa is intact. My family back in Bangladesh do not know any of this happened, and that is the way it will stay.

## What I want to remember

That I called 988 during that week. I do not want to dress it up. I had a moment where I genuinely could not see how I would walk forward, and the part of me that has always wanted to live picked up the phone. I am still here. I am writing on a Thursday morning in Toledo and the light is good and I made a painting last night.

That my advisor at the university, who I will not name beyond his role, became the person on the inside who moved my case. He did not have to. I had been a difficult student to advocate for — my GPA had dropped, I had too many things going on, I had taken on too much. He took the case anyway. There are people in institutions whose job is to be a door, and most of them are not, and some of them are. He was.

That my friend Britni at the gallery in Toledo had hugged me a week before the suspension landed and told me my work was beautiful. I did not understand at the time how much I would replay that hug in the days that came after.

That I kept painting through it. Every day or every other day, even when nothing else made sense. Six new pieces in a week. Some of them are the best work I have made.

## What this season took

Energy. A specific kind of energy that does not come back overnight. I am writing this slowly. I have been told to rest and I am trying to rest, and rest turns out to be a skill I am bad at.

It took some illusions about myself. The illusion that I could carry a senior-level course load, a thirteen-person studio in Dhaka, two research papers, a poster, a relationship, a gallery practice, and a family I was not telling the truth to — all at once. I cannot. Nobody can. The math was always going to break, and it broke.

It took some illusions about the world too. I was always told the United States was a meritocracy and that if you worked hard you would be alright. The version I lived this season is different. The version I lived this season is one where I did work hard and where I almost lost everything anyway, and the only thing that saved me was that a few specific people, on no schedule, decided to help. I am grateful to those people. I am also clear-eyed about the system that made it possible for one mistake to cost everything.

## What I am keeping

I am keeping the work. The paintings I made during the worst weeks are not therapy — they are paintings. They have their own logic and their own pull. I am keeping the AIES paper I submitted at the bottom of that crisis. I am keeping the research with Dr. Ray. I am keeping Afroza. I am keeping the friends in Toledo and Dhaka who showed up.

I am letting some things go. The double life with my family is one I will figure out in its own time, not on a deadline. The idea that I have to be three people at once is gone. The plan was never the plan. The plan, it turns out, was just to be alive on a Thursday in May and to have the light be this good.

## What is next

Fall is twelve credits, one of which is a recovery class called Rocket Recharge that I am genuinely looking forward to. Monthly meetings with the success office. A GPA floor I will not approach, let alone touch. I am aiming higher than the floor. That part is for me, not for the conditions.

The summer is paintings. The summer is the AI immigration assistant I want to build. The summer is showing up to the studio and to the keyboard and to the people I love, in that order. The summer is rest, which is the part I am worst at, and which is the part I most need.

If you are reading this and you are anywhere near the place I was three weeks ago — the place where the math feels like it cannot be made to work — please pick up the phone. The number is 988 in the United States. There is a version of your story where you also write the piece from the other side. I would like to read it.

— *Toledo, May 2026*
