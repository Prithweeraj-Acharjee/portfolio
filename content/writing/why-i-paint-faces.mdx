I am working on a body of paintings right now where the face sits in the middle of the canvas and the rest of the canvas is allowed to riot around it. This is not a portrait practice. The faces are not anybody. They are barely faces. Sometimes they are a mass of charcoal and tempera that I have scratched at until something looks back.

I want to write down why I keep doing this, because the next time I sit in front of a fresh canvas I will forget.

## The face is the thing that decides whether the painting works

For about a year I made work that did not have a face in it. Some of it was good — collage work, photographs, surfaces I was happy with. But I never knew when a piece was finished. I would keep adding and the painting would keep absorbing what I added, and at some point I would walk away and call it done because I was tired.

When the face is there, the painting tells me when it is finished. Not because the face is precious — usually the face is the most violently treated part of the canvas — but because once a face is on a surface, every other mark has to negotiate with it. The drips choose to go around the eye or through it. The colors choose to push the head forward or let it sink. There is somebody to argue with.

## Scribbling is the opposite of drawing

I went to a school where drawing meant fine pencil lines and an eraser. I was bad at it. I was bad at it because the slow careful line is not how I see — I see in bursts, things at the edge of vision, things half-remembered, things from photographs I scrolled past on a phone. The slow careful line was lying about what was actually in my head.

Scribbling tells the truth. A scribble is what the hand does when it stops asking for permission. Charcoal on top of tempera on top of acrylic on top of spray, scratched into with a key — none of those marks are decisions, exactly. They are speeds. The painting is a record of how fast my hand was moving when it touched the surface.

## What the drape does

A lot of the recent pieces have something falling. Cloth, hair, paint itself — something coming down from above the face, hanging on it, partly covering it. I think this is the central image of the series. A face is being prevented from looking at you, or is being protected from being looked at, depending on which side of the drape you stand on.

I grew up in a country where women in my family kept their hair and their skin and their bodies under cloth most of the time. That is part of where the drape comes from. The other part is the work I made earlier about being watched — the *Freedom Under* series — and the realization that being covered and being free are not opposites. Sometimes the drape is what lets the face stay alive.

## Things I am trying to figure out

How big these should be. The recent ones are four feet by six feet, which is the biggest I have worked, and I think they want to be bigger. There is a moment in the studio where the canvas has gotten large enough that I have to step back to see it, and the painting begins to do what it does in a gallery — it lives at the size of a person.

How many of them there should be. Eight feels right for the first show. Twelve feels right for the second.

How to talk about them without overexplaining. The thing about a face on a canvas is that everybody already knows what it is. I do not have to write a wall text saying "this is a meditation on the gaze." The face does that work. The wall text just needs to not get in the way.

## The next painting

I am supposed to be resting. I am also planning to be in the studio tomorrow. The next painting will be larger and the face will be on the right side, looking left, and the left side of the canvas will be a field of dark cloth coming down. I do not know what color the face will be yet. I will know when I have the paint in my hand.

— *Toledo*
